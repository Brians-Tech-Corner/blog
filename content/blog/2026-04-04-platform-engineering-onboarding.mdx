# Building a Platform Engineering Onboarding Program That Actually Works

You're the new Lead at a company. Your team is about to grow. Or maybe you're joining as a new platform engineer and the onboarding is chaos. Either way, you've realized: **platform engineering onboarding is different from regular engineering onboarding.**

Why? Because platform engineers need context across systems. They need to understand infrastructure, deployment pipelines, cost models, reliability metrics, and team dynamics—all while being useful on day one.

I'm doing this right now at my new role. Here's what I'm learning.

---

## The Problem

Bad onboarding for platform teams looks like:

- Day 1: "Here's Slack, here's our GitHub. Figure it out."
- Week 1: 47 different Confluence docs, none current
- Week 2: You're in meetings about systems you don't understand
- Week 3: You touch production and break something because nobody explained the gotchas
- Month 2: You're still not sure what the actual priorities are

Good onboarding is intentional. It's a 30-day plan with clear milestones.

---

## Week 1: Get Your Bearings

### First 72 Hours Checklist

- [ ] **Access**: AWS console, Kubernetes clusters, CI/CD platform, VPN, internal tools
- [ ] **Documentation**: Current architecture diagrams, runbooks, on-call playbooks
- [ ] **Relationships**: Meet your team lead, key stakeholders, customer contacts
- [ ] **First task**: Something *low-risk but visible* (docs, small bug fix, ops task)

**Why this order?** You need access before you can do anything. You need docs before you understand what you're doing. You need relationships before you need them in an emergency.

### Day 1 Meeting Template

Ask your manager:
1. "What's the top infrastructure pain point you're hiring me to solve?"
2. "What should I absolutely not touch in my first month?"
3. "Who's the tribal knowledge keeper for [system]?"
4. "What's your definition of success for my first 90 days?"

Write down the answers. You'll reference them constantly.

### Documentation Sprint

Assign your week-1 afternoon to:
- [ ] Read the current architecture overview (30 mins)
- [ ] Skim the runbooks for common incidents (30 mins)
- [ ] Map out your current tech stack (services, databases, deployment targets)
- [ ] Identify what's *outdated* (mark for updates)

**Pro tip:** When you find docs are wrong, that's a data point. Track it. You'll fix these in week 2.

---

## Week 2-3: Build Muscle Memory

### Systems Audit

Pick 3 critical systems:
1. The deployment pipeline (how code gets to production)
2. The observability system (how you know things are broken)
3. The incident response process (what happens when it all goes wrong)

For each, create a one-pager:
- What is it?
- Why do we use it?
- How do you access it?
- Common issues and fixes
- Who's the expert?

This becomes your reference guide *and* your study material.

### First Code Change

Don't touch the main code yet. Instead:
- Documentation fix (update a README, fix a broken link)
- CI/CD improvement (add a missing check, fix a flaky test)
- Ops task (update a runbook, fix a monitoring alert)

**Why?** You learn the deployment process without risking production. And your PRs get reviewed, which surfaces the team's standards.

### Meetings You Should Be In

- Architecture discussion (weekly)
- On-call handoff (if applicable)
- Sprint planning (see the workflow)
- One-on-ones with engineers (learn their perspective)

**Meetings to skip initially:**
- Strategic planning (you don't have context yet)
- Quarterly reviews (not your domain)
- Sales engineering calls (unless you're customer-facing)

## Weeks 3-4: Prove You Can Help

### First Real Project

Pick something that's:
- **Medium complexity** (not trivial, not critical)
- **Has clear success criteria** (you'll know when you're done)
- **Improves something** (the team sees value)

Examples:
- Set up a staging environment that actually mirrors production
- Write a disaster recovery runbook and test it
- Add cost monitoring for a service
- Automate a manual ops process

**Why this timing?** You have enough context to not be dangerous. You're confident enough to ask good questions. The team's ready to trust your work.

### Blocker Escalation

By week 3, you'll be stuck on something. **Good.** That's where you find:
- Knowledge gaps (learn from the team)
- Process gaps (candidate for improvement)
- Documentation gaps (mark for fixes)

When you're blocked:
1. Try to unblock yourself (check docs, search Slack history)
2. Ask one person (preferably your manager or tech lead)
3. If still blocked after 30 mins, escalate

This teaches you the team's communication patterns.

## First 30 Days: Metrics That Matter

Track these:

- **Accessibility**: Can you run the main services locally? Can you deploy? (Week 1 → Week 2)
- **Context**: Can you explain the architecture in a diagram? (Week 2 → Week 3)
- **Contribution**: Can you unblock yourself and help others? (Week 3 → Week 4)
- **Credibility**: Does the team trust your judgment on technical decisions? (Ongoing)

By day 30, you should be:
- ✅ Fully able to deploy and roll back
- ✅ Understanding the major architectural decisions
- ✅ Making small improvements without supervision
- ✅ Known for asking smart questions

## Common Pitfalls (That I've Seen)

### 1. **Rushing Into Big Changes**

You see something broken. You want to fix it immediately. *Don't.* You don't know why it's that way. Ask first.

**Better:** Document the issue, propose a solution, get buy-in, then execute.

### 2. **Skipping the Relationship Building**

You think: "I'll just focus on the work." Wrong. The work *depends* on relationships. Who do you call when production is down? Who can grant access? Who knows where the tribal knowledge is?

**Better:** Coffee chats, ask questions, let people know you're earnest.

### 3. **Information Overload**

Everything is new. You're reading docs, sitting in meetings, taking on tasks, trying to understand the architecture *simultaneously*.

**Better:** One thing at a time. Week 1 = access + docs. Week 2 = systems audit. Week 3+ = contribution.

### 4. **Not Documenting Your Learning**

You figure something out. You think, "I'll remember this." You won't. And the next person will be confused too.

**Better:** Every time you learn something, write it down. Update docs. Share it.

## Your 30-Day Roadmap Template

```
Week 1: Get access, read docs, meet the team
  - Goal: Understand what you've signed up for
  - Win: Deploy a test to staging
  - Blocker: Can't access something

Week 2: Understand critical systems
  - Goal: Know how code gets to prod, how you detect issues, what happens in an emergency
  - Win: Write a one-pager on each system
  - Blocker: Docs are outdated

Week 3: Make your first contribution
  - Goal: Prove you can help
  - Win: Merge a meaningful PR (code, docs, or ops)
  - Blocker: Architecture questions

Week 4: Get unblocked and help unblock others
  - Goal: Become a resource
  - Win: Resolve a team question or improve a process
  - Blocker: Missing access or knowledge
```

## For Managers: How to Onboard Someone

If you're hiring a platform engineer:

1. **Prepare the environment** (access, docs, sandbox)
2. **Assign a buddy** (someone who's been here >6 months)
3. **Have a plan** (this one, customized to your team)
4. **Check in daily week 1**, weekly weeks 2-4
5. **Celebrate small wins** (they're learning fast under pressure)
6. **Course-correct early** (if something's confusing, fix it now)

The time you invest in onboarding pays dividends in retention, confidence, and cultural fit.

## The Real Goal

You're not trying to be productive on day one. You're trying to be *effective in 30 days*.

That means:
- Understanding the systems
- Building relationships
- Establishing trust
- Identifying where you can help

Everything after that is momentum.
